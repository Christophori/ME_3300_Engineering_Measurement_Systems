\section{Learning objective}
The objectives of this experiment are to provide students with an opportunity to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Become more familiar with using MATLAB for data acquisition, analysis, and visualization and sharing (e.g., plotting, etc.).
    \item Design, build, and test a non-inverting amplifier circuit using a generic operational amplifier chip.
    \item Design, build, and test an instrumentation amplifier circuit using an instrumentation amplifier chip.
    \item Explore and verify the performance characteristics of operational amplifiers.
    \item Verify the gains of the constructed op-amp circuits.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Introduction}
In this experiment, you will construct and test two op-amp circuits. The first will be the non-inverting amplifier shown in figure \ref{fig:opAmp}, using National Instruments’ LM358 DIP (dual in-line package) chip. The second will be an instrumentation amplifier circuit using Analog Devices’ AD622 DIP. Both circuits will be built to achieve a gain of approximately G = 3.  Details for both of these DIP chips are given at the end of this document.

\section{Laboratory instruction}
The goal of this experiment is to learn about the operational amplifier and setting the gain in the op-amp circuit.

\subsection{Part 1: Construct and test a non-inverting amplifier}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Construct a non-inverting amplifier circuit on a breadboard.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Verify the correct color bands using a resistor chart (or internet resource).
        \item Measure and record the actual resistor values using your DMM.
        \item Use your measured values to calculate expected gain.
        \item Reference the LM358 datasheet to learn how to connect the DIP chip. See section \ref{sec:instrument}.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Place the op-amp on the breadboard across the center channel as seen in Figure \ref{fig:opAmp}.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item NOTE: ensure that the op-amp is properly positioned on the breadboard (push down on the chip; it should not wiggle).
    \end{itemize}
    \item Construct your circuit on the breadboard in a neat and organized manner. 
    \item Use the long vertical rails for your common signals (such as the +15V, -15V, ground, etc.).
    \item Use the NI MyDAQ power supply to provide +15V and -15V to the chip at the V+ (pin 8) and GND (pin 4) pins, respectively. 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item NOTE: the GND (pin 4) pin on the LM358 amplifier is not the signal ground, it should be -15V for this experiment
    \end{itemize}
    \item Connect your resistors as shown in figure \ref{fig:opAmp}, with $R_1$ = 10 k$\Omega$ and $R_2$ = 20 k$\Omega$.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $G=\left(\frac{R_1+R_2}{R_2} \right)$
    \end{itemize}
    \item Use the Analog out ``AO 0" on the NI MyDAQ for supplying the input voltage to your op-amp circuit \\ (use a range of 0-5 Volts for input). 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Update the MATLAB code \boldpurp{MATLAB\_Data\_Acquisition.m} so that ``AO 0" provides a voltage.
    \end{itemize}
    \item Use the handheld digital multimeter to test the non-inverting op-amp circuit to verify that it is performing correctly.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Yellow wires: ground 
        \item Blue wires: $V^{-}_{supply}$ 
        \item Red wires: $V^{+}_{supply}$ 
        \item Green wires: $V_{out}$ output voltage signal, acquired at ``AI0" of NI-MyDAQ
        \item Orange wires: $V_{in}$, input voltage signal, generated from ``AO0" of NI MyDAQ and acquired at ``AI1'' of NI MyDAQ to record the data.
\end{itemize}
    \item Show your circuit to TAs before acquiring the data. {\bf improper wiring will damage the op-amp}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{OP-AMP LM358}
\label{sec:LM358}
The LM358 DIP (dual in-line package) actually includes two internal operational amplifiers. For this experiment, you will be using only 1 side (A or B) of the chip.  A connection diagram is given in Figure  \ref{fig:SchematicLM358}. To determine which side is A and which is B, locate the half-circle indentation on the top of the chip and match it to the diagram. The chip must be powered by an external $\pm$12 or $\pm$15 Volt external power supply (for this lab, we will use the powered breadboard or similar). The +15 Volt source should be connected to the V+ pin, and the -15 Volt source should be connected to the GND pin. Please note that the GND pin should not be connected to the signal ground! Make sure, however, that the ground from the input signal is connected to the ground from the powered breadboard, and that you measure the output voltage from pin 1 or pin 7 (depending on which side you use) to the common ground (not the GND pin!). A detailed datasheet for the LM358N can be found at: \href{https://www.sparkfun.com/datasheets/Components/General/LM358.pdf}{Sparkfun website}.

\subsection{Instrument amplifier AD-622}
\label{sec:instrument}
The AD622 DIP (dual in-line package) contains several operational amplifiers arranged in an instrumentation amplifier circuit utilizing precision resistors in order to improve the accuracy of the gain and to reduce uncertainty.  A connection diagram is shown in Figure \ref{fig:SchematicAD622}.  Refer to the datasheet below for determining how to change the gain through the external resistor $R_G$ The AD622 datasheet can be found at:  \href{http://www.analog.com/static/imported-files/data_sheets/AD622.pdf}{AD622 Webpage}.
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=3.00in]{AD622.png}
\caption{Connection diagram and package outline for the AD622 instrumentation amplifier.}
\label{fig:SchematicAD622}
\end{figure}

The AD622 gain is resistor programmed by RG or, more precisely, by whatever impedance appears between Pin 1 and Pin 8. The AD622 is designed to offer gains as close as possible to popular integer values using standard 1\% resistors. Figure \ref{fig:TableAD622} shows required values of RG for various gains. Note that for
G = 1, the RG pins are unconnected (RG = $\infty$). For any arbitrary gain, RG can be calculated by using the formula
\begin{equation}
R_G = \frac{50.5k\Omega}{G-1}
\end{equation}
To minimize gain error, avoid high parasitic resistance in series with $R_G$. To minimize gain drift, RG should have a low temperature coefficient less than 10 ppm/$^o$C for the best performance.
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=4.00in]{AD622Table.png}
\caption{Required Values of Gain Resistor.}
\label{fig:TableAD622}
\end{figure}
